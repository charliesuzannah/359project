How are drones used?

Drones have been part of warfare since the 1930s. They are defined as any unmanned aircraft. The first iteration was primarily used as a remote-controlled target for anti-aircraft gunners to use for target practice. Since then, they have been used for many military objectives from spying to targeted assassinations. They are frequently used to avoid risking the lives of military members and reduce the need for ground operations.

Drone technology is used by many countries, though primarily by the United States, in the following ways:

1. Strategically with unilateral constraint: Countries use strikes to attack terrorists without deploying boots on the ground (see US in Pakistan).


2. Strategically with multilateral constraint: The constant presence of drones above other countries, threatening to undermine their sovereignty (see US in Libya).


3. Tactically with unilateral constraint: Predatory strikes, often used in intrastate conflicts/ territorial disputes (see Ethiopia).


4. Tactically with multilateral constraint: Limited strikes are multilaterally approved (France likes doing this).



The US frequently uses drones, particularly in the “war on terror.” The use of drones has expanded as the technology has improved. The US uses drones partly to decrease the risk to American lives, and partly so that it does not have to declare war on a country to attack them. The problem with this process is that it is then largely unsupervised, and leaders are not held to account for the fallout when civilians are killed.
